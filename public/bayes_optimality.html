Decision Theory

Suppose you have decided you want to start investing — what portfolio should you choose, right now, to maximise your returns? This has the makings of a classic decision problem in statistics, characterised by a situation where we observe some data \(X\) under a state of the world \(\theta\), and must choose an action \(a\). In our example, \(X\) would correspond to the set of past asset prices, \(\theta\) a parameter indexing the (joint) distribution of past and, importantly, future asset prices, and we must choose a proportion \(a\) for each asset to hold in the overall portfolio.

How do we quantify the success, or failure, of our decision \(a\)? By minimising our loss — in this example, the negative of our income generated by the portfolio. Notice how this loss depends not only on our action, but on the realisation of future states, i.e. future asset prices. We would imagine, then, that the optimal decision would take into account the probabilities of each asset price appreciating and depreciating by varying amounts. More generally, we wish to minimise a loss function \(L(a, \theta)\) that depends both on the action \(a\), chosen via a decision rule \(a = \delta(X)\) applied to observed data, and on the state of the world \(\theta\).

What decision function \(\delta\) best minimises loss, or results in the best portfolio returns? Well, say we pick the rule "invest 100% into Apple stock". That might seem sub-optimal, but if the future state of the world is one in which Apple stock is the only asset that appreciates, then we actually managed to pick the optimal decision function. In all other states, however, this rule is sub-optimal.

Therefore we need some way of quantifying the trade-off of varying decision-induced loss over states of the world. This is what the risk function captures:

\[ R(\delta,\, \theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\!\left[\, L\!\left(\delta(X),\, \theta\right)\right]. \]

In words, risk captures the expected loss from a given decision function, where the expectation is taken over the distribution of data \(X\) under state \(\theta\). It tells us how well — or how poorly — a rule \(\delta\) performs in a given world state, on average.

Bayesian Optimality

It should be obvious that a small risk is a desirable property of a good decision function - we don't wish to make portfolio allocation decisions that lose a lot of money a lot of the time. But it is not the case, necessarily, that the decision function that minimises risk is optimal. How do we rank preferences for risk vs. returns, for example?

One criterion favoured by many economists is the Bayesian optimality approach - one simply assigns a prior probability \(\pi\), or weight, to each state of the world. If you believe a particular state to be more likely than another, you care more about that state relative to others. This idea is captured within integrated risk:

\[ R(\delta,\, \pi) \;=\; \int R(\delta,\, \theta)\, \pi(\theta)\, \mathrm{d}\theta. \]

The optimal decision in this Bayesian setting is simply the decision that minimises this integrated risk. We have now created a complete ranking system that is able to obtain any admissible decision (I've cheated a little here by implicitly assuming the regularity conditions required by Wald's Complete Class Theorem).

Admissibility

What is an admissible decision? If you have studied any economics at an undergraduate level, you are already familiar with the concept, just under a different name - Pareto dominance. This is why the above graph may have looked familiar to you.

A decision is admissible if there are no decisions that dominate it. A strategy is said to dominate another if its associated risk is at least as low as another strategy for all states of the world, and results in strictly lower risk for at least one state of the world. The most powerful result, arguably, for the Bayes optimality approach is that every admissible decision function can be recovered simply by altering the prior weights for each state. If you can make good prior assessment of the probability of future asset price returns, then the Bayesian optimality criteria will lead you to optimal asset allocation.
